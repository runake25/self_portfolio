## The Three Principles

Before any framework, three truths you need to internalize fully.

### 1. You cannot win every hand — under any circumstance

# TODO
Suppose you have `K♠️Q♠️` and flop comes `A♦️8♣️5♣️`, it is disrespectful to even think your skill can overcome the fact that you missed your hand. The hand is not in your odds anymore, and you need to re-evaluate . The goal is to be right *more often* and to lose *less* when you're wrong.

<div class="note"><strong>Note —</strong> "Wrong" here refers to a decision that deviated from your prior expectation — not necessarily a -EV play.</div>

### 2. Preflop decisions dictate the hand

Think of poker like a gunfight. Your preflop hand is your weapon — and even the best shooter struggles when handed a broken gun. Play junk preflop and you're already fighting at a disadvantage. Worse, when you pull the trigger, nobody flinches — they know your gun is broken.

22%, that's the number of hands you should be opening when you act first on preflop. By removing the bottom 50-75% of hands, you are helping yourself tremendously in a few ways:
- You are less likely to be exploited by better players
- You avoid tough decisions preflop & postflop with weak holdings
- You build a tighter image, making your bluffs more credible


<div class="note"><strong>Note —</strong> To put matter into perspective, you have a 30x EV advantage facing others with a top 20% range (0.11 vs 3.11)</div>

### 3. Ask yourself questions — you will figure it out

I really like this one and I wish to write a whole article to share about it, but I will try to illustrate the importance of it before your attention goes away. 

I believe even a beginner can come up answer their own doubts if they ask the right questions. You should train yourself extensively to ask the right question in the right order (*not only in poker but also in life !*). Let say you are facing a scenario:

    You are holding `2♠️3♠️`, after betting flop and turn you are now on the river with `A♦️8♠️5♠️K♥️7♦️`. Opponent checks to you, what do you do?

Well clearly there are only 2 options, fold, to give up, or bluff to try to win the pot. Most beginner will dabble between the like this:

    "Should I bluff here? But what if he has a strong hand? But what if he is weak? But what if he never folds? But what if ...
  
These are all valid questions, but they are not bring you to anywhere, **if you dont go for one of them**. Try treating them as a rope and climb it with everything you have:


    "Should I bluff here? But what if he has a strong hand? Does he look like he has a strong hand? Does those strong hand play like this? How about weak hands? Does he has more weak hand play like this compare with the strong hand ..."

    "Ok I think he has more weak hand that play like this, so I will bluff here. But what if he never folds? Does he look like a player that never folds? Does he have history of folding in similar spot?"

The more you ask, the more you will find your answer. They might not be the right answer, and most of the time they are not, but they are your answer, with logical reasoning behind it, and that is what matter. This feedback loop serves as a training for your intuition, and is way superior than blind solver studies (although they are costly when you lose) for a couple reasons:
- It is active, the strong feedback loop will help you memorize the pattern and the reasoning behind it, which is what you need to make decision in real time.
- The chain of thought serve as a perfect study point for you to review and improve later on, you can study those key heuristic questions and correct them if you misestimate any part.



<div class="tip"><strong>Tip —</strong> When you're stuck on a decision at the table, verbalize it internally as a question. "What hands call here that I beat?" forces clearer reasoning than "should I bet?"</div>

## Math vs. Abstraction

Here's an easy way to illustrate the distinction.

**Math** tells you: calling a pot-sized bet requires 33% equity to break even.

**Abstraction** tells you: does your hand actually have 33% equity against *this specific player's range in this specific spot*? And even if it does — should you be folding to apply pressure elsewhere, or calling to disguise your hand strength?

The math is the same for everyone at the table. The abstraction is where edges are made.

| Layer | What it answers |
|---|---|
| Math | Is this call +EV in a vacuum? |
| Range reading | Against *who* and *what*? |
| Abstraction | What story am I telling? What's the metagame? |

---

## Takeaway

Most losing players know the math. The gap is almost never in the numbers — it's in the framing. Build the habit of asking questions over looking for answers, and the answers will follow.
